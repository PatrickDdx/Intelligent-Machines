Introduction

1. Hook / Key Result

Start strong — what did you discover or prove?

Example:

My model revealed a clear divide between smokers and non-smokers — so distinct that you could almost draw a line through the data. What began as a simple prediction task turned into an exploration of how lifestyle, age, and health risk shape real-world costs.

Prompt:

In one or two sentences, describe your most interesting finding or surprise.

2. Why It Matters

Significance? Explain the real-world relevance briefly.

Predicting [target variable] isn’t just an academic challenge — it’s central to how [industry or people] make decisions about [outcome]. These insights help [who] [do what].

Prompt:

Why does this [prediction / classification / model / analysis] matter? Who benefits or learns something from it? Why is this development important to the field or to users.

3. The Data

Describe the dataset briefly (size, features, target). Keep it to a single paragraph or short list. Include the most informative initial visualization (e.g., a distribution plot).

Example:

The dataset contains [number] observations, each describing [what the rows represent]. Key features include [list 4–6 features briefly]. The target variable is [target], which ranges from [low] to [high].

Prompt:

Describe dataset, main columns, and link if public. (Source: Dataset on Kaggle)

Methods & Results

4. What I Did

Summarize your workflow and models quickly.

Example:

The workflow followed a simple pattern: EDA → Feature Engineering → Model Training → Evaluation. I cleaned categorical data, encoded features, and compared Linear Regression, Random Forest, and Gradient Boosting models. Performance was evaluated using MAE and R² scores.

Prompt:

Summarize your overall pipeline in 3–4 lines — from raw data to results. Write sentences about your process, models, and metrics.

5. What I Found

Use short bullet points or 3–5 sentences to highlight insights.

Prompt:

Write your top 3–5 findings or surprises. Mention trends, relationships, or model outcomes.

Conclusion

6. What I Learned

Reflect on the project — personal or technical takeaway.

This project reminded me that [lesson]. Even small datasets can reveal rich stories when you focus on [what]. I also learned that [technical or conceptual insight].

Prompt:

What did this project teach you about data, modeling, or problem-solving?

7. What’s Next

Close with momentum — a sentence about your next project or curiosity.

Next, I plan to apply the same thinking to [next dataset/problem] — shifting from [type of task] to [type of task].

Prompt:

Mention your upcoming project or what you want to explore next.

Quick Style Rules

• Paragraphs: Keep them short—ideally 3-4 lines, unless explicitly told otherwise.

• Tone: Human and insight-driven. Use plain language, but do not shy away from bolding and clearly defining key technical terms (e.g., RMSE, Feature Importance, One-Hot Encoding).

• Include 1-2 highly relevant, clearly captioned charts or images for every main section (3, 4, 5). Else one chart or image per section if possible.

• Use bold for key terms or numbers.

• Target Length: 600-800 words
